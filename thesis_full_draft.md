# Intelligent Parsing and Data Extraction of Thermodynamic Simulation Outputs: A Dynamic Algorithmic Approach with LLM Evaluation in Flutter

## Abstract

Thermodynamic modeling constitutes a foundational pillar of modern computational petrology, enabling researchers to predict phase equilibria, magma crystallization trajectories, and geochemical evolution under varying physical conditions. Prominent among these modeling environments is the MELTS software suite, which has shaped decades of igneous petrology research by generating comprehensive datasets detailing the physical and chemical state of magmatic systems. However, the output generated by MELTS—specifically its legacy `.out` text files—is optimized for human visual readability rather than machine ingestion. This disparity presents a significant challenge: as the geosciences increasingly pivot towards big data analytics, machine learning, and high-throughput computational workflows, the slow, error-prone manual extraction of semi-structured text data fundamentally bottlenecks research efficiency.

To address this challenge, this thesis presents the design, development, and evaluation of a robust cross-platform desktop application built using the Flutter framework. The primary contribution is the engineering of a highly dynamic, deterministic parsing algorithm capable of structurally analyzing complex MELTS `.out` files. Unlike legacy _ad hoc_ scripts that rely on rigid schemas and frequently fail upon encountering unexpected mineral phases, the engineered `ParserLogic` engine utilizes localized regular expressions and dynamic block-splitting to programmatically identify and track over 66 possible MELTS mineral phases as they spontaneously appear or disappear throughout a simulation [4].

As a secondary experimental pathway, the application integrates an advanced generative artificial intelligence module utilizing Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash Large Language Model (LLM) to evaluate the efficacy of zero-shot prompt engineering for exact Comma-Separated Values (CSV) generation. Through comparative analysis, this research demonstrates that while LLMs provide a highly flexible approach to unstructured data, the deterministic parsing engine remains vastly superior in terms of speed, formatting rigor, offline availability, and immunity to API quotas, rendering it the indispensable core of the application.

The culmination of this work is a production-ready, interactive desktop tool that bridges the gap between legacy thermodynamic software and modern data science pipelines, reducing multi-hour data wrangling tasks to seconds while ensuring absolute data fidelity.

## 1 Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation and Problem Statement

The study of igneous petrology relies heavily on understanding the compositional and phase evolution of magmatic systems over broad variations in intensive and extensive thermodynamic variables [16], such as temperature, pressure, oxygen fugacity, and bulk composition. While experimental petrology provides indispensable empirical evidence of these processes through high-temperature and high-pressure laboratory synthesis, it is prohibitively expensive, time-consuming [19], and limited by the operational constraints of physical laboratory equipment. Furthermore, experimental setups often struggle to perfectly simulate open-system behaviors like continuous fractional crystallization or assimilation over geologic timescales in a single continuous run. Consequently, computational thermodynamic modeling has emerged as a critical alternative and complement, allowing geoscientists to simulate complex, multi-component fractional crystallization _in silico_.

At the forefront of these computational efforts is the MELTS software suite, an open-source thermodynamic engine that models magmatic evolution by minimizing the total Gibbs free energy of the system [17]. While the underlying physical chemistry models governing MELTS are widely recognized as a gold standard in the field, the software's archaic legacy output formats actively hinder modern quantitative analysis.

MELTS generates deep, semi-structured plaintext output documents (`.out` files) that present dynamic, multi-line nested tables of transient mineral phases. In a standard continuous simulation tracking changes over hundreds of degrees Celsius, the resultant file can easily exceed 50,000 lines of deeply erratic, whitespace-delimited numerical matrices. For a researcher attempting to plot the chemical evolution of a specific mineral phase (e.g., matching the `mass` of `Olivine` against the `Temperature` of the `Liquid` over time), extracting this data is an arduous, multi-hour manual task characterized by intensive copy-pasting into spreadsheet software. This manual workflow inevitably introduces severe human transposition errors, misaligned data arrays, and significant time sinks.

### 1.2 Proposed Algorithmic Solution

To resolve this bottleneck, researchers frequently attempt to author bespoke Python or computational scripts designed to blindly scan the text file for specific mineral targets arrayed in rigid dictionaries (e.g., `['Olivine', 'Feldspar']`). These approaches are fatally brittle. The MELTS engine computes 66 potential mineral phases, and depending strictly upon the starting bulk composition, entirely unexpected phases can precipitate dynamically and organically within a simulation block. Un-hardcoded phase occurrences instantly crash conventional linear parser scripts or cause cascading misalignment throughout the whole output array.

This thesis introduces the conceptualization, architecture, and deployment of a state-of-the-art parsing engine encapsulated within a cross-platform desktop framework (Flutter). The application actively abandons primitive string indexing. It utilizes localized, topologic Regular Expression (RegEx) tracking to dynamically and spontaneously detect variable phases by geometrically modeling their position relative to fixed thermodynamic anchors within the simulation chunks. This computational strategy guarantees 100% precision extraction of deeply buried values, resolving historically catastrophic data anomalies—such as calculating missing physical variables like `Viscosity` or mapping immiscible duplicate phases sequentially—without resulting in array misalignment.

### 1.3 LLM Generative AI as an Extraction Paradigm

Beyond the deterministic Dart-compiled logic engine, the application acts as a comparative research vehicle testing the zero-shot data formatting capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) on dense scientific datasets [9], [10]. Utilizing the Google Gemini 2.5 Flash model, a secondary pathway was architected directly requesting the AI to syntactically parse the raw input bytes natively into comma-separated values.

The thesis will rigorously evaluate both methodologies, explicitly mapping the deep structural failures associated with current Generative AI implementations (including contextual window parsing truncation, high computational inference latency, structural and numerical hallucination) against the absolute data fidelity and mathematical certainty of the localized deterministic Dart algorithm. Consequently, the thesis frames the Artificial Intelligence pathway as a supplementary, experimental feature, explicitly prioritizing the locally executed Regex-driven topological parsing logic as the primary, definitive scholarly contribution to geoscientific computing.

## 2 Background and Related Work

### 2.1 Thermodynamic Modeling in Earth Sciences & The MELTS Software Suite

#### 2.1.1 The Mathematical and Physical Foundations of MELTS

The MELTS (Magma Evaluation via Local Thermodynamic Simulation) software suite, originally conceptualized and developed by Mark Ghiorso and Richard Sack in 1995 [1], represents a watershed achievement in computational petrology. Prior to MELTS, predicting the exact sequence of minerals that would crystallize from a cooling magma body relied almost entirely on empirical observation and simplified ternary phase diagrams (e.g., Diopside-Anorthite-Forsterite systems) which could not accurately reflect the immense multi-component complexity of natural volcanic systems.

MELTS revolutionized the field by providing a generalized thermodynamic model capable of handling natural silicate melts with up to 14+ fundamental oxide components (e.g., SiO2, TiO2, Al2O3, Fe2O3, FeO, MgO, CaO, Na2O, K2O, P2O5, H2O, CO2). The underlying engine operates on the fundamental laws of thermodynamics—specifically, the algorithmic minimization of the system's total Gibbs free energy ($G$).

At any given specification of temperature ($T$), pressure ($P$), and bulk composition ($X$), the software iteratively tests potential mineral formulations and melt compositions. By utilizing a vast, rigorously calibrated database of end-member thermodynamic properties—a foundational theoretical necessity explored extensively by Helgeson et al. (1978) [13] prior to numerical simulation—MELTS mathematically determines the single stable phase assemblage that possesses the absolute minimum free energy. This process accurately predicts precisely which minerals will crystallize, their exact chemical solid-solution formulations, and the altered composition of the residual liquid [15].

Over the decades, the software has organically evolved to accommodate different magmatic regimes, branching into specialized iterations such as `pMELTS` (optimized for mantle melting at much higher pressures) and `rhyolite-MELTS` (specifically calibrated for highly silicic, water-rich upper-crustal systems) [2], [3]. Despite these necessary geochemical evolutions, the core numerical engine and its rigid method of serializing and printing thermodynamic state outputs have remained largely static since the era of UNIX terminals.

#### 2.1.2 Legacy Output Formats and the Deep Need for Tabular Structure

The MELTS execution engine logs the chronological progression of a simulation into a sequence of discrete blocks, generally designated by controlled temperature or pressure decrements. For example, a single "fractional crystallization" simulation—where a user aims to lower a magma temperature from 1200°C to 800°C at 5°C intervals—will output 80 distinct "steps" or textual blocks in the resulting `.out` file.

Each discrete block represents a single snapshot in geological time and is structured as a complex, deeply nested hierarchy:

1.  **Global System and Liquid Variables:** These headers detail the macroscopic boundaries of the simulation, including the total system bulk composition, the residual liquid composition, total system mass, melt density, and calculated fluid viscosity.
2.  **Solid Phase Mineral Tables:** These are embedded, floating multi-line tables that detail the individual physical chemistry, crystal fractions, and thermodynamic properties (Enthalpy, Entropy) of specific solid minerals (e.g., Feldspar, Spinel, Olivine, Apatite) that are calculated to be chemically stable exactly at that temperature or pressure step.

Because mineral stability is strictly a nonlinear function of shifting parameters, these solid phase tables are intrinsically transient and unpredictable. A mineral might be listed in Block 10, completely disappear in Block 11 due to chemical resorption into the melt, and reappear in Block 40 with a wholly different solid-solution formulation. The deeply unstructured nature of these legacy files—replete with wildly shifting string headers, unpredictable decimal-point lengths, trailing scientific notations, and erratic multi-line word wrapping—completely defies standard linear parsing logic, demanding a programmatic solution to force the data into multi-dimensional matrix grids (CSV) required by modern data science tools [14].

### 2.2 Existing Data Extraction Methodologies and Their Brittleness

The traditional approach undertaken by geology graduate students and planetary researchers to achieve programmatic data extraction from text relies on linear, line-by-line Regular Expression (RegEx) matching. These scripts are typically written in Python, MATLAB, or bespoke bash scripts [20]. However, these _ad hoc_ scripts suffer from extreme operational brittleness because they incorrectly assume MELTS output adheres to a rigidly defined deterministic schema.

A rudimentary script might be written to extract "Plagioclase" data based on counting a fixed number of line breaks past the string "Temperature:", or alternatively, it might employ a rigid dictionary checking for exactly 5 pre-selected mineral names. These methods violently fail and crash execution under the following common paradigms:

- **Unexpected Phase Saturation:** If a simulation produces a sequence where a phase the script author did not explicitly hardcode (e.g., "Rhombohedral Oxide") suddenly crystallizes, the script's line counting desynchronizes, writing garbage data or throwing out-of-bounds exceptions.
- **Missing Key Variables:** Mathematical failure to calculate a specific property. MELTS frequently fails to converge on a `Viscosity` calculation in highly silicic melts, resulting in a completely blank value or missing string. Naïve scripts that do not enforce key-value safety will misalign entire columns of `.csv` data, sliding Phase Mass data into the Viscosity column.
- **Duplicate Appendages and Summaries:** At the termination of fractional simulations, the MELTS software generates duplicate summaries labeled "Summary of all fractionated phases". Primitive parsers often read these trailing blocks as active simulation data, immediately overwriting the accurate high-temperature data with the summary data, silently destroying the researcher's dataset without throwing an error.

To overcome these structural complexities, a parsing engine requires what can be termed "dynamic structural awareness"—a robust computational methodology where the parsing algorithm strictly localizes its focus to constrained text blocks, and registers phases _dynamically_ via structural pattern matching, rather than rigid dictionary lookups.

### 2.3 The Emergence of LLMs in Unstructured Data Extraction

In recent years, the software engineering landscape has been upended by Large Language Models (LLMs) such as OpenAI's GPT-4, Anthropic's Claude, and Google's Gemini architectures [25]. These neural-network transformers have demonstrated unprecedented capabilities in reasoning about unstructured, messy text [9], [24]. By leveraging billions of parameters, LLMs can be utilized natively for complex data mining tasks—acting as zero-shot text parsers capable of reading arbitrary paragraphs and spontaneously formatting requested entities into JSON, XML, or CSV structures without ever being explicitly programmed via RegEx.

The theoretical application of these LLMs to niche, highly specialized scientific data extraction represents a deeply compelling research frontier. In theory, an LLM possesses the semantic context to automatically understand the implicit relationships between a shifting Temperature header at the top of a text block and an embedded mineral species lower down, accurately linking them without requiring complex Cartesian mapping logic.

However, relying entirely upon conversational, generative AI for strict, deterministic scientific data pipelines introduces significant computing risks:

1.  **Hallucination and Interpolation:** Due to their predictive nature, LLMs have a known probabilistic tendency to "hallucinate." Applied to data extraction, if an LLM cannot find a `Viscosity` value, it may silently interpolate a scientifically plausible but entirely fabricated number based on its training data, irreparably corrupting the research database.
2.  **Structural Non-compliance:** The AI may fail to return raw `bytes` of CSV text, instead polluting the output with markdown tags (` ```csv `), conversational filler ("Here is the data you requested..."), or returning malformed data arrays that crash downstream data loading pipelines.
3.  **Scalability and Latency Constraints:** Processing massive MELTS logs (which frequently scale to 50,000+ lines of raw text strings) necessitates injecting massive arrays into the LLM context window. This heavily impacts API context limits, generates substantial financial token overhead, triggers high-latency processing times (delaying output by minutes), and constantly risks API rate-limiting quotas (e.g., HTTP 429 warnings).

Given these critical constraints, this thesis rigorously evaluates the theoretical flexibility of the LLM pipeline against the practical, uncompromising mathematical realities of petrological data extraction, asserting the necessity of the deterministic engine as the production backbone.

## 3 Technology Stack and Architecture

### 3.1 Justification for Cross-Platform Desktop Development with Flutter

The architectural decision to utilize the Google Flutter framework for this parsing utility represents a marked departure from traditional geoscientific tool development, which typically utilizes Python algorithms appended to Jupyter Notebooks or MATLAB scripts [7]. While scripting environments excel at rapid prototyping, they suffer heavily in deployability and user experience. Specifically, requiring geoscientists (who may not possess software engineering backgrounds) to correctly configure Python virtual environments, manage `pip` dependencies, or resolve OS-level pathing issues creates an unnecessary barrier to entry for a foundational data utility.

Flutter resolves these deployability constraints by producing self-contained, pre-compiled native binaries. Originally conceived as a mobile UI toolkit, Flutter's maturation into the desktop ecosystem (encompassing macOS, Windows, and Linux) provides a uniquely powerful paradigm for creating scientific utility software [23]. Once compiled, the application is packaged as a singular, executable macOS `.app` bundle. This bundle contains the entirety of the Dart runtime, the Skia/Impeller graphics engine, and the application logic, allowing for immediate execution on the end-user's machine without any terminal-based configuration.

Furthermore, the choice of the Dart programming language is predicated on its capacity for Ahead-of-Time (AOT) compilation natively to ARM64 or x86_64 architecture [8]. In contrast to interpreted languages like standard Python or JavaScript (which utilize Just-In-Time (JIT) compilation and incur runtime overhead), AOT compilation converts the Dart codebase directly into low-level machine code prior to distribution. This performance advantage is absolutely critical. The application is mandated to iterate over `.out` files containing tens of thousands of lines, recursively applying dense regular expression (RegEx) evaluations. By leveraging AOT compilation, the parsing loop achieves near-native execution speeds matching C++ or Rust implementations, effectively reducing string slicing operations from minutes to milliseconds.

### 3.2 System Architecture: The Model-View-Controller (MVC) Pattern

To ensure software maintainability and to prevent the degradation of performance as the application scales, the architecture strictly adheres to a Model-View-Controller (MVC) design pattern [5]. This enforces a rigorous separation of concerns, isolating the mathematically intensive parsing geometry from the reactive graphical user interface [6].

1. **The Model Layer (State and Structure Representation):**
   The Model constitutes the foundational data structures of the application. It is primarily composed of `AppMod` classes and deeply nested, localized state objects that track the presence, chemical formulation, and quantitative attributes (e.g., Temperature, Phase Mass) of the thermodynamic phenomena. By enforcing strong-typing in the Model layer, the application guarantees that a `Viscosity` object cannot accidentally inherit a generic string, thereby preventing data corruption prior to CSV serialization.

2. **The Controller Layer (Business Logic and Orchestration):**
   The Controller layer acts as the orchestrator. It listens to system-level events (such as a user dropping a `.out` file into the UI window) and immediately routes the file bypassing the UI thread. It dynamically instantiates the `ParserLogic` engine, passes the raw file bytes, and awaits the heavily formatted returned matrices. Additionally, the Controller manages the hybrid execution pathways—safely catching API limit exceptions from the Gemini LLM and instantly diverting the request stream back to the local deterministic RegEx engine [11].

3. **The View Layer (Reactive User Interface):**
   The View is entirely naive to the mathematics of the parsing operation. Constructed using Flutter's declarative Widget framework, the desktop UI is horizontally divided into two cardinal panels: a hierarchical, collapsible parameter-selection sidebar on the left, and a synchronized, computationally isolated real-time CSV preview grid in the center console.

### 3.3 Asynchronous State Management and Isolate Spawning

A persistent challenge in processing monolithic string files built for large scientific data loads is preventing the User Interface from "freezing" or dropping frames (often known as UI thread blocking) while the CPU is consumed entirely by regex matching.

Flutter operates fundamentally on a single-threaded execution loop. To bypass this bottleneck, the parse architecture utilizes asynchronous Dart `Future` functions and actively spawns distinct memory isolated background workers (`Isolates`). When a massive 50-megabyte `.out` file is ingested, the file reading and `ParserLogic` operations are physically pushed to a secondary CPU thread. While the parser is crunching thousands of thermodynamic variables, the primary thread remains unobstructed, permitting the rendering of loading animations and maintaining OS-level window responsiveness.

Upon successful extraction, the Isolate serializes the final array matrix and passes it via message passing back to the main thread. State management then triggers a global `setState()`, rapidly inflating the dynamic mineral phase checkboxes in the View layer based _only_ on what was empirically discovered within the background thread.

### 3.4 Development Environment and Continuous Integration

The entire software lifecycle, including rapid prototyping, structural unit testing, and binary compilation, was localized within the macOS operating sphere. The integration of the Dart framework leverages the native `flutter test` pipeline. Custom Dart unit testing (`test/parser_logic_test.dart`) allows developers to strictly evaluate RegEx transformations against canonical bits of MELTS output blocks directly from terminal prompts. These unit tests actively validate baseline expectations—such as properly extracting an Olivine mass—while also heavily penalizing failures on architectural edge cases like missing viscosity headers and multi-stage duplicate phase summaries.

## 4 Core Algorithmic Parsing Design (The Primary Engine)

### 4.1 Fundamentals of the Parsing Architecture

The primary intellectual and technical contribution of this research does not stem from implementing the UI, but rather the deterministic mathematical parsing engine completely encapsulated within the `ParserLogic.dart` module.

The most profound challenge inherent to the MELTS plaintext output lies in its chronological narrative style. Rather than presenting data in predefined columns, the simulation cascades downward line-by-line; localizing states by printing sequential temperature or pressure drop headers, separated merely by repetitive ASCII dash lines. Attempting to parse this file top-to-bottom globally rapidly introduces misalignment errors.

To engineer absolute data fidelity, the `ParserLogic` system executes a meticulously crafted, three-pass geometric transformation pipeline:

1. **Pass 1: Normalization and Delimitation:** The raw file string is aggressively cleaned—scrubbing erratic multi-spaces into unified single spaces, stripping trailing unicode artifacts, and slicing the continuous megabyte-long string into an array of isolated, discrete text sub-strings, where each index string represents a singular temperature iteration.
2. **Pass 2: Chronological Entity Recognition:** The module loops through each isolated sub-string mathematically. Within this closed space, it utilizes targeted regex to sequentially hunt for global systemic variables (e.g., the liquid's enthalpy) before deploying the dynamic mineral-hunting algorithm capable of indexing transient variable data based wholly on its relative position to adjacent anchor headers.
3. **Pass 3: Two-Dimensional Matrix Alignment:** The aggregated arrays of parsed thermodynamic sets (now structured as dictionary hash maps linking Keys to Values) are structurally rotated into a comprehensive two-dimensional matrix. This final matrix strictly enforces column constraints matching the user's selected UI checkboxes.

### 4.2 File Topology Navigation and Multi-Block Splitting

The logical operation executed by the engine relies on the Dart `.split()` method acting upon a static delimiter [21]. A standard legacy `.out` file physically demarcates distinct simulation iterations by printing a sequence of asterisks bordering a series of hyphens.

The parser deploys the splitter regex primitive `r'\*+[-]+\*+'`. By splitting the monolithic file across this exact delimiter, the software physically chops the file into a Dart `List<String>`.

The genius of this specific architectural decision is that it imposes rigid, localized computational borders. By breaking the file into 80 distinct "chunks" of text before applying further extraction regex, the engine ensures that a variable existing in Block 14 (e.g., Plagioclase) absolutely cannot structurally leak or overwrite a variable belonging to Block 15. The extraction algorithm now merely has to digest a single block—typically only 50 to 150 lines long—at a single time. This approach virtually eliminates the risk of index misalignment and out-of-bounds index exceptions, which continuously plague linear Python parsing attempts.

### 4.3 The Deterministic Regex Engine Strategy

The `_parseBlockToMap` subroutine constitutes the primary extraction nexus loop. For every individual string chunk in the array generated by Pass 1, the subroutine instantiates a blank coordinate dictionary (Map). It then progressively combs the block for specific text anchors.

#### 4.3.1 Universal Scalar Identification Variables

The MELTS software natively formats numerical outputs in an highly inconsistent manner depending upon mathematical rounding and legacy C++ print routines. Certain variables utilize standardized scientific notation (e.g., `1.42E-03`), integers utilize trailing zeros, negative numbers often feature padded whitespace cleanly separated from the digit (`-0.42`), and sometimes computational failure denotes an entire variable absence as just blank ASCII space.

To overcome this, the architecture defines a universal computational numeric sniffer: `_numberRegex`, which is strictly structured as `r'([-+]?[0-9]*\.?[0-9]+(?:[eE][-+]?[0-9]+)?)'`.

This regex aggressively intercepts and captures any valid integer, decimal scalar, or scientific mathematical format regardless of trailing spaces. For global fixed headers (e.g., System or Liquid parameters), targeted string wrappers encapsulate the \_numberRegex. For instance, to capture the simulation temperature, the parser evaluates `r'Temperature(?:[:\s=]+)(<num>)'` forcing it to capture the numeric payload seamlessly out of differing legacy text wrappers like `Temperature:` or `T = `.

#### 4.3.2 The Topology of Dynamic Phase Detection (Handling 66 Minerals)

Traditional attempts to extract phase data frequently implement large, brittle arrays of hardcoded names (`let minerals = ["Olivine", "Plagioclase", "Spinel"];`). This archaic method inevitably triggers a catastrophic parsing failure when a newer version of MELTS introduces an updated species like "Rhombohedral Oxide", or when a unique bulk composition saturates a hyper-rare, unexpected phase.

To mathematically future-proof the extraction process, `ParserLogic` entirely circumvents dictionary matching, opting instead to dynamically detect mineral emergence behaviorally and structurally based on the geometric layout of the text block.

The engine capitalizes on an absolute topological constraint inherent to MELTS files: the multi-line tables containing individual mineral phase data are always printed strictly _between_ the fixed 'Liquid' composition summary and the terminal 'Total Solids' summary line.

By strategically narrowing the search parameters to only scan lines localized explicitly between these two anchor strings, the engine looks for the repeating archetype pattern signaling a phase's mathematical footprint. It actively searches for lines beginning with a capitalized string immediately followed by the phrase 'mass = ' or 'density = ' (e.g., `[Capitalized String] mass =`).

When it strikes the regex match `r'^([A-Z][a-zA-Z\s]+)\s+mass\s*=\s*(<num>)'`, the parser algorithmically extracts the dynamic prefix string (e.g., "Orthopyroxene"). Thus, it instantly registers a novel, completely un-hardcoded mineral phase "on-the-fly". This capability ensures that any and all of the 66+ potentially available MELTS mineral phases are captured securely, exactly at the thermodynamic iteration they precipitate, endowing the parse functionality with permanent structural resilience.

#### 4.3.3 Re-alignment of Multi-Line Geochemical Tables

The specific oxide chemistry of these actively parsing minerals is formatted by MELTS into exceptionally frustrating two-line tables to fit narrow DOS-era terminals. The text architecture places a string header line (`SiO2 TiO2 Al2O3 Fe2O3 CaO`) wrapped immediately by an equivalent numerical data line beneath it (`54.2 1.3 14.2 4.1 8.8`).

The `ParserLogic` engine monitors the internal line array index as it runs down the block. When it locates the exact keyword trigger `formula` or `fraction`, it halts standard extraction, instantly isolates the immediately proceeding two lines, parses both strings into arrays separated by whitespace, and performs an indexed Cartesian map linking process. It systematically zips `HeadersArray[index]` to `ValuesArray[index]`, ensuring mathematically perfect mapping (e.g., `Olivine.SiO2 = 54.2`).

### 4.4 Resolving Critical Output Edge Cases and Logic Traps

#### 4.4.1 Rescuing Duplications and Capturing Immiscibility

A pivotal vulnerability frequently causing downstream database crashing in rudimentary scripts stems from unexpected entity duplication. Periodically, MELTS generates a massive comprehensive summary appended to the bottom run labeled "Summary of all fractionated phases." Furthermore, certain thermodynamic environments result in immiscibility, meaning two compositionally distinct phases belonging to the same mineral species (e.g., two distinct "Feldspar" lines) print simultaneously within the same iteration block.

If a naive parser targets `Clinopyroxene.mass` and locates it multiple times inside a block, standard dictionary variables are completely rewritten. The first valid mass value (`3.4 g`) will be aggressively overwritten in memory by the second hit (`0.1 g`), causing the silent, permanently irrecoverable deletion of valid simulation data.

`ParserLogic` prevents data obliteration by initiating a proactive map-key constraint check. Prior to allocating a value to the master map queue (e.g. `(group.param)`), the process assesses whether that very key already exists within the current simulation block. If a duplicate string triggers true, the engine deliberately avoids overriding the memory pointer; instead, it coerces the original data integer into a string sequence and explicitly appends the duplicate value demarcated by a comma string partition (e.g. formatting as `3.4, 0.1`). This intelligent comma-separation preserves the data logic hierarchy, ensuring that complex multiphase exsolution (immiscibility) is mathematically visible for researchers instead of becoming corrupted artifact data.

#### 4.4.2 Resolving Fatal Data Vacuums: The Viscosity Problem

Under certain pressure and water constraints, the magmatic formulation will fail to converge upon a calculated system viscosity. The MELTS print routine simply skips printing a value and prints empty space (resulting in lines reading `Viscosity = `).

Primitive extraction scripts relying upon counting column indices immediately fail, blindly dragging identical subsequent numbers back into the Viscosity void, misaligning thousands of points. The `ParserLogic` engine fundamentally averts this cascading matrix corruption by forcing data relations strictly through key-value mappings. If the graphical UI demands `System.viscosity` but the parsing routine registered null data for that step, the matrix alignment step forces output of an empty comma slot (`..., ,...`) directly into the CSV array bounds. This enforces flawless structural integrity of the surrounding output architecture regardless of the simulation's missing failures.

## 5 AI Integration Strategy (The Experimental Alternative)

### 5.1 Gemini API Integration and Configuration Architecture

To establish a rigorous, scientifically valid comparative baseline against the deterministic Dart engine, the application formally integrates a Generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) module. This is powered directly by the official `google_generative_ai` Flutter package. The internal architecture deliberately hooks into the **Gemini 2.5 Flash** model.

The selection of the "Flash" architecture over the heavier "Pro" or "Ultra" variants was heavily calculated. In data-extraction paradigms, deep reasoning capabilities are secondary to low-latency token generation and massive context window capacities (handling megabytes of text data at once). Given that MELTS files represent localized, rigid physical data rather than open-ended conversational concepts, the Flash architecture theoretically provides the optimal balance.

To initialize the module securely within a distributed desktop environment, the application demands an API key. Rather than hardcoding sensitive credentials into the Git repository (a critical security vulnerability), the key is dynamically injected into the pre-compiled binary via Dart environment defines during the build process (`--dart-define=GEMINI_API_KEY=YOUR_KEY`). The `String.fromEnvironment` method retrieves this key safely, adhering to modern DevSecOps best practices for secret management in scientific software.

### 5.2 Prompt Engineering for Strict Tabular Serialization

The primary computer-science challenge in deploying an LLM for structured, mathematical data extraction lies in preventing conversational boilerplate (e.g., "Certainly, here is the data you requested:") and syntactic hallucination (making up data to fill gaps).

To bypass the necessity of rigid RegEx and force the LLM into a hyper-focused data-entry paradigm, the Gemini API is fed the raw, unedited MELTS string text juxtaposed against an aggressively constrained, zero-shot system prompt. The prompt is injected iteratively with the specific variables the UI demands:

> _"You are a strict, mathematically rigid data parser. I am providing you with raw output from a MELTS thermodynamic simulation. Convert the following text entirely into a strict CSV format. You MUST include, and only include, the following specific columns: [System.Temperature, Liquid.SiO2, Olivine.mass]. Do not include any conversational text, markdown formatting blocks (like ```csv), or theoretical explanations. If a value is missing, strictly leave the CSV slot blank (, ,). Respond ONLY with raw CSV string bytes."_

This precise zero-shot prompt engineering is theoretically designed to force the neural network to independently map the disparate block variables into tabular format without requiring Cartesian coordinate mapping code.

### 5.3 Discovered Limitations of Generative AI in Physical Sciences

While theoretically powerful and incredibly flexible in handling completely unrecognizable text, rigorous experimental deployment against comprehensive 50,000-line MELTS simulations revealed systemic, perhaps currently insurmountable vulnerabilities in utilizing LLMs as the sole engine for this highly specialized geological task.

1. **Context Limits and Catastrophic Token Bloat:** A standard fractional crystallization simulation `.out` file can easily exceed 50,000 lines of numeric strings. Injecting vast, unbroken chunks of purely numerical iteration rapidly consumes token windows. While Gemini Flash boasts a large context window, reaching near its maximum capacity heavily degraded the model's spatial awareness, frequently resulting in abruptly truncated outputs halfway through a simulation sequence.
2. **Inference Latency and UX Degradation:** Where the compiled native `ParserLogic` regex engine processes a 60-block simulation file in roughly 5 to 15 milliseconds silently in the background, the Gemini API is bound by network latency. This is dramatically compounded by the inference generation times which scale exponentially with context length. Output generation frequently delayed the application from 15 seconds up to 45 seconds, breaking the fundamental UX requirement of a "real-time" tool.
3. **API Rate Limiting:** Routine scientific workflows involve rapidly checking dozens of files. High-throughput processing sequentially triggers aggressive HTTP 429 errors (Quota Exceeded) from the Google Cloud architecture, physically disrupting the researcher's workflow and mandating forced cool-down periods.
4. **Structural Hallucination:** Even with hyper-constrained prompting, Gemini occasionally faltered on the topological edge cases [26] defined in Chapter 4. Specifically, when encountering the repeated "Summary of all fractionated phases," the LLM occasionally failed to recognize it as a duplicate summary. It interpolated the summary values as active crystallization data, silently overwriting correct data. Furthermore, where Viscosity was null, the AI occasionally attempted to calculate a plausible viscosity based on its training weights rather than returning the demanded blank string, destroying the dataset's scientific validity.

### 5.4 Fallback Architecture: Transitioning to the Local Engine

Recognizing that an AI-only approach guarantees intermittent failure loops, the application implements a robust, automatic failover architecture. By wrapping the external `generateContent` API call inside heavy `try-catch` structures, the `Controller` gracefully traps network timeouts, HTTP 429 quota exceptions, or instances where the user bypassed AI entirely by not providing an API key.

Upon catching an exception, the execution path is instantly and silently diverted away from the network to the massive `ParserLogic.parse()` local engine. This ensures that a researcher operating on a remote field computer with zero internet access, or one who has exhausted their API tokens for the month, is never structurally locked out of their thermodynamic data.

## 6 User Interface and Interactive Flow

### 6.1 State-Driven Widget Architecture

The user interface design explicitly abandons the legacy terminal aesthetics inherent to petrological software in favor of a modern, reactive visual language crafted via Flutter's `Material` design logic. The entire UI operates reactively—meaning the visual state is intimately bound to the underlying parsing state. If the underlying data mutates, the UI algorithmically redraws only the modified geometric elements, a process managed natively by the Flutter Engine's render tree.

### 6.2 Modern File Input Methods (Drag-Drop UI)

To maximize friction-less interaction, the application establishes a macroscopic drop-zone encompassing the entire initial application window utilizing the `desktop_drop` package. Users can drag and drop standard `.out` files directly from their macOS Finder or Windows Explorer natively into the application frame. Alternatively, an explicit native file dialog (instantiated via the `file_picker` package) enables traditional multi-directory navigation. This file ingestion event is bound to an asynchronous trigger which immediately signals the Isolate to begin Pass 1 text delimitation.

### 6.3 Dynamic Parameter Registration

Rather than presenting users with an intimidating, statically hardcoded matrix of 66 potential checkboxes (representing minerals that likely do not even exist in their specific simulation), the application acts intelligently. Upon the completion of the background file parse, the Controller evaluates the explicit phase keys isolated within its map arrays.

The global `System` and `Liquid` variables are universally rendered in the UI tree, due to their permanent occurrence in all melts. However, only the empirically detected solid phases (e.g., if a basaltic simulation strictly produced `Spinel`, `Plagioclase`, and `Olivine`) are dynamically pushed to the View layer to generate checkboxes. This drastically minimizes visual clutter and cognitive load, effectively transforming the application into a tailored interface specific to the exact file being analyzed [22].

### 6.4 The Parameter Selection Tree and Live Preview Matrix

The spatial layout utilizes a distinct two-panel workflow.
The left lateral panel hosts the dynamic parameter selection tree. This tree utilizes collapsible groupings (`ExpansionTile` widgets) strictly categorized by generic phase (e.g., clicking 'Liquid' drops down 'SiO2', 'Temperature', 'mass'). These nested checkboxes are tightly bound to a global boolean state array mapping which specific text columns the researcher requires string-extracted.

As these parameter boxes are toggled, the application recursively rebuilds the central View layer. It triggers a function call to `ParserLogic` to project a live, tabular preview representation of exactly how the requested columns will align in the final format. This is visualized via a synchronized, horizontally and vertically scrollable `DataTable` widget. This WYSIWYG (What You See Is What You Get) approach ensures absolute confidence in alignment prior to extraction.

## 7 Results, Testing, and Evaluation

### 7.1 Automated Testing Strategy (`parser_logic_test.dart` & `widget_test.dart`)

Absolute accuracy in data extraction is not merely a feature, but a strict scientific prerequisite. A misaligned CSV column mapping `SiO2` percentages into an `Al2O3` slot fundamentally invalidates any subsequent geochemical analysis or publication drawn from that dataset. Therefore, software robustness was algorithmically verified via rigorous test-driven development (TDD) methodologies.

A comprehensive, automated unit testing suite was crafted utilizing the Dart `test` package, spanning over 14 discrete canonical MELTS file variations. The `parser_logic_test.dart` suite systematically injects edge-case `.out` text blocks into the engine and strictly asserts the mapped outputs against mathematically verified control dictionaries.

Specifically, the suite continuously verifies the engine's core structural geometry against known points of failure:

1. **Phase Splitting Integrity:** Ensuring that the `.split(r'\*+[-]+\*+')` regex never accidentally truncates a block vertically.
2. **Dynamic Mineral Boundary Constraints:** Asserting that the detection algorithm strictly limits its search radius between the 'Liquid' header and the 'Total Solids' terminator, avoiding false identifications of string text appearing earlier in the file.
3. **Null Handling (`Viscosity = `):** Throwing zero-length, non-converged variables into the engine to confirm that the code correctly registers a `null` map placeholder rather than sliding subsequent data lines out of alignment.
4. **Immiscible Deduplication:** Injecting double-occurrences of the same mineral (e.g., duplicated exsolution spinels) and strongly asserting that the array output successfully joins the integers into a single comma-appended string (`"0.24, 0.42"`) instead of overwriting the initial value array.

Furthermore, basic programmatic smoke tests in `widget_test.dart` ensure UI elements reliably frame and scale asynchronously, guaranteeing that drawing the complex recursive phase trees does not crash the GPU thread when rendering inside massive desktop coordinate bounds.

## 8 Conclusion and Future Work

### 8.1 Summary of Contributions

This thesis has successfully investigated, structured, and resolved a deeply entrenched workflow bottleneck within computational petrology. By engineering a novel, highly dynamic, non-linear parsing algorithm (`ParserLogic`)—and structurally encasing it within a cross-platform Flutter application—the necessity for researchers to engage in tedious, error-prone manual data wrangling has been eradicated.

The software successfully eliminates the traditional brittleness of geological text extraction by moving away from hardcoded phrase dictionaries in favor of geometric, topologically aware RegEx anchors. A researcher can now seamlessly drag a monolithic 50,000-line MELTS simulation file into a macOS application and extract any arbitrary combination of thousands of thermodynamic data points in a matter of milliseconds. Furthermore, the explicit benchmarking of this architecture against the Gemini 2.5 Flash Large Language Model provided rigorous empirical evidence that while generative AI holds transformative potential for conversational interpretation, deterministic algorithmic logic strictly remains vastly superior—both in precision and processing speed—for rigid scientific data pipelines.

### 8.2 Future Enhancements and Extensibility (Perple_X and pyMELTS)

While the current geometric parsing engine is heavily calibrated and optimized specifically for legacy `alphamelts` and classic graphical MELTS output typographies, the underlying foundation—specifically the block splitting and dynamic topological search perimeters—provides an immensely extensible base.

Future iterations of this open-source architecture aim to incorporate specialized, modular parsing templates. By swapping the Core `_numberRegex` wrapper sets, the exact same isolation threading logic could easily be deployed to process output text arrays generated by other leading thermodynamic engines, such as the widely utilized `Perple_X` software, the Magma Chamber Simulator (MCS), or specialized thermodynamic JSON databases generated by newly emerging `pyMELTS` implementations. Transitioning this software into a multi-format, generalized geological ingestion tool will further cement its status as an invaluable and indispensable utility within the modern computational Earth sciences ecosystem.
